# Mindset Deep Dive (When It Feels Hard)

Per the VAIB poll, 83% of students say the real reason they haven't been doing warm outreach is that it feels awkward or embarrassing to message friends and family. This is the actual problem this whole repo exists to solve.

Read this when you're stuck, or when a student tells you they just can't bring themselves to send.

## The three breaks (in depth)

### 1. Targets are anyone

Most people assume warm outreach means "warm leads for your business" and default to messaging business owners. It doesn't.

Warm outreach means anyone who'd recognize your name. Your cousin. Your old flatmate. Your mum's friend. Your gym buddy. None of them need to be a business owner, because they're not the buyer. They're the bridge. Your mum's friend from book club has a son who runs a clinic. Your cousin coaches a team whose parents own businesses. You don't know who they know until you talk.

If a student keeps saying "but they're not in my target market", they've misunderstood the premise. There is no target market in warm outreach. There's only your people, and their people.

### 2. You are not selling anything

This is the one that's hardest to internalize. Every student wants to believe they can thread the needle, say the right thing, turn the warm conversation into a client. That's the sales voice leaking in.

The point of a warm conversation is **not** to close the warm contact. It's to build goodwill with someone who knows you, and to eventually ask for a referral to someone who doesn't. The two legitimate actions are:

- **Showing off what you do** when they ask. Not pitching, showing. "Here's this cool thing I built, it does X". No call to action, no offer.
- **Asking for a referral** once goodwill is built. "Anyone come to mind?". If no, move on.

That's the whole sales motion in warm outreach. If a student is trying to do anything else, they'll sound like a salesperson and the conversation will die.

### 3. Actually doing it is the hardest part

Everything else is an excuse. "I don't know what to say" (you do, the tool drafts it). "I need to pick a niche first" (you don't, see `no-niches.md`). "My network isn't big enough" (it is, see `small-network.md`). "I want to get the demo perfect" (the current one is fine).

The real blocker is pressing send on a message to someone you know. It feels like you're imposing, like you're asking for something, like you're going to look weird.

You're not. You're texting someone you know and asking how they are. That's normal human behavior. The discomfort is a story your brain is telling you that isn't actually true.

## Lines to send to yourself when you're stuck

Save these. Use them.

- "this is literally just a text to a friend, the AI part doesn't make it weird"
- "the worst realistic outcome is they don't reply, and i've survived that 10,000 times"
- "if they think it's weird, they were never going to refer anyone anyway"
- "my job isn't to close them, it's to be in touch. that's a low bar"
- "everyone else feels the same awkwardness. the ones who push through win"

## If a student just can't send

The softest way forward: send the message to one person who will definitely reply warmly. A sibling. A parent. A best friend. Someone where rejection is zero risk. Just to prove the muscle works. Then the second is easier, the third is trivial.

If even that fails, we have a bigger problem than drafting quality and `/coach` probably isn't enough, they need a coach-coach conversation offline.

## The Carnegie underpinning

Why does this work at all? Because of what Carnegie observed a century ago: people have a deep, under-met desire to feel important. Messaging someone and noticing something real about them is a gift, not an imposition. You are giving them 30 seconds of being seen. That is almost always welcome.

See `.claude/skills/outreach/reference/carnegie.md` for the philosophical bedrock. Read it when a student is convinced warm outreach is self-serving. It isn't, if you do it right.
