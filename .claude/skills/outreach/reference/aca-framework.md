# ACA Framework

A structure for crossing relational distance in a warm first message. NOT a universal template.

## When to use full ACA

ACA is scaffolding. Scaffolding exists because there's a gap to cross. The bigger the gap between you and the contact right now (in time, frequency, closeness), the more ACA structure helps. The smaller the gap, the less you need any of it.

Rough guide:

- **Close (best mate, family, weekly-ish contact)**: skip ACA structure entirely. Just text them like a human. Full ACA reads like you're re-introducing yourself to your own sister.
- **Friend with active thread**: pick up where you left off. Acknowledge from the thread, a light Ask. Compliment only if something genuinely impressive happened.
- **Friend, gone quiet (6+ months no contact)**: light ACA. Acknowledge + Ask always, Compliment if you've got a real trigger.
- **Acquaintance with active thread** (old coworker who you exchanged a few messages with last year): medium ACA. The thread does some of the Acknowledge work. Add an update about you + one Ask.
- **Acquaintance, cold-ish** (old classmate you haven't spoken to in 3+ years, friend-of-a-friend, ex-colleague long gone): **full ACA**. This is the canonical use case. A + C + A, all three, every time.

The `/outreach` skill picks the cell automatically from the tier you set during the flow + whether there's an active thread. Don't apply full ACA to every contact, it mismatches the relationship.

## The Three Parts

**Acknowledge**, Notice something real about them. Not generic. Something you could only say if you actually paid attention to their life: a recent post, a job change, a project, a trip, a kid, a hobby.

**Compliment**, Genuine, specific praise tied to the acknowledgement. Not "you're awesome." Something like "most people gave up after like 2 weeks, you actually committed." It has to feel earned.

**Ask**, One open question that makes replying effortless. Yes/no questions are fine as long as they invite continuation. The goal is to get them talking about themselves, not to lead anywhere. No selling, no offer teasing, no product mention.

## Rules

1. **Lead with their name.** Never `{{firstName}}`. Use the actual name.
2. **Short.** No huge paragraphs. Think text message length, not email length.
3. **Conversational tone.** Match how you'd actually text this person. If you'd say "yo Chris!" say "yo Chris!" not "Dear Christopher."
4. **No product mention.** Zero voice AI, zero agency, zero "by the way." The first message is purely a catchup.
5. **Easy to reply to.** Make it so easy that ignoring you feels weirder than replying.
6. **One question, not three.** Don't stack questions. Give them one thing to answer.

## What Counts as Warm

**Warm (ACA applies):**
- Friends (current and old)
- Family (including extended, cousins, uncles, aunts, in-laws)
- Former coworkers and bosses
- Classmates (school, uni, courses, bootcamps)
- Neighbors
- People you've actually met at events, weddings, parties
- Past clients or customers
- Friends-of-friends with real introduction
- Community members you've interacted with (not just passive followers)

**NOT warm (do not use this skill at all):**
- LinkedIn connections you've never messaged
- Instagram followers you don't personally know
- "Second degree" connections
- People from scraped lists
- Anyone you'd have to Google to remember
- "Random" people from networking events you don't actually remember

This skill is warm-only. If the person isn't warm, stop. Don't generate anything.

## The Hook is the Acknowledge

The whole message lives or dies on the Acknowledge. If you can't find something real to acknowledge, you either don't know this person well enough (not warm) or you haven't looked at their profile/life closely enough. Spend 60 seconds scrolling before you write.

Hooks that work:
- Recent post (especially wins, travel, career moves)
- Shared history ("we met at Sarah's wedding last year")
- Known fact about them ("heard you're coaching youth soccer now")
- Life event (new job, new city, new baby, promotion)
- Specific thing they built or made

Hooks that don't:
- Generic "hope you're doing well"
- "Saw your profile" (creepy, not an acknowledge)
- "How's everything?"
- Anything that could apply to 1000 people

## If the Acknowledge Hook is Thin

If you genuinely can't find anything to acknowledge, the fall-back is the connection itself: "Hey [name], it's [your name] from [specific shared context]." But always try for a real hook first.
