# Catchup Techniques (how to actually talk to people)

Read this before drafting ANY reply in REPLY mode. The whole point of warm outreach is genuine conversation. Students tend to pivot to work the moment they get a reply, which kills the warm frame. These techniques keep the conversation on THEM until they've genuinely earned the right to ask about you.

This file is for introverts and technically-minded people who feel awkward doing small talk. You don't need natural charisma. You need a scaffold.

## The four techniques that do the work

### 1. FORD (Family, Occupation, Recreation, Dreams)

When you're stuck for what to ask next, cycle through these four buckets. Pick whichever hasn't been covered yet. You always have something to ask about.

- **Family**: siblings, kids, parents, partner, where they grew up
- **Occupation**: current job, how they got into it, what they actually do day-to-day
- **Recreation**: hobbies, sports, weekends, trips, what they're watching/reading
- **Dreams**: what they want next, something they've been meaning to do, a project they've been on

You're not interrogating. You're picking one bucket per turn and asking a follow-up tied to something they already said. Example: they mention they moved cities, you ask "recreation" bucket ("what's the local scene like for running there?") not "dreams" bucket ("so what's the five-year plan?"). Match the bucket to the thread.

### 2. Mirror-and-pause

The single lowest-effort technique that works every time.

When they say something, repeat the last two or three words back as a question. Then shut up. They will expand.

Example:
> Them: "yeah I moved to Berlin last year for work"
> You: "for work?"
> Them: [explains the whole situation]

It feels too simple to work. It works. It works because humans love being asked to elaborate on their own life, and mirroring signals you were actually listening. Use this when you don't know what to ask next. It's a free turn.

### 3. Two or three follow-ups on the SAME topic

Your instinct when they reply will be to switch topics. Fight it.

If they mention running, ask three questions about running before moving on. Not "how was your trip? nice. what are you watching?". Do "how was your trip? → what was the highlight? → would you go back?".

Research on rapport-building shows stacked follow-ups build more connection than breadth. People feel heard when you stay on their topic. They feel scanned when you jump.

Practical rule: don't change topic until the thread has naturally died (they give a one-word reply and don't add anything). Let the conversation actually breathe on one subject.

### 4. Don't equate (Celeste Headlee)

When they share something, don't match it with your own version.

> Bad: "Oh I also went to Italy once, we had the best pasta at..."
> Good: "no way, what part? how was it?"

The moment you turn their story into your story, the conversation becomes about you. Let their thing be their thing. Ask about it.

This is especially tempting when you have genuinely shared experience. Suppress it. If it's relevant, bring it back much later after they've had their full turn.

## How to respond to generic greetings WITHOUT pivoting to work

These are the replies that students usually screw up. Here are drop-in responses that keep the conversation on them.

### "hey how's it going?" / "how are you?"

Keep yours one line max. Do NOT elaborate. Then pivot back with a specific question tied to something you know about them.

- "pretty good, been busy with stuff. how about you, still [thing they do]?"
- "can't complain tbh. more curious what you've been up to, last I knew you were [context]"
- "all good! how's [their recent thing] going?"

Notice: no mention of work. "been busy with stuff" is plenty. They didn't ask for detail, they were being polite.

### "been ages! what have you been up to?"

Generic, polite, NOT a genuine work question. Deflect warmly.

- "haha yeah way too long. honestly just head-down on stuff, how about you? last I saw you were [context]"
- "bit of everything honestly, same old same old. but tell me about you, still [their thing]?"
- "quite a lot actually haha, bore you with it later. what's going on with you, [specific hook]?"

### "what's new with you?"

- "not a ton haha, few things cooking. more interested in you though, what's new on your end?"
- "bit of a weird year honestly. how about you, still [their context]?"

### "so what have you been doing since [old context]?"

This one's trickier because they've flagged interest. Still deflect the first pass.

- "oh man, a bit of everything. took a while to figure out what I wanted to do, but that's a boring story. you still in [their world]?"
- "few pivots haha, it's a saga. what about you, are you still [thing]?"

### "what are you up to these days?"

Most ambiguous. Could be polite or genuine. Default to deflection first time. If they ask again → Stage 1.

- "honestly just grinding on a few things, nothing wild. how about you?"
- "bit of this bit of that. what's going on with you, still at [place]?"

## Rules of thumb for REPLY mode

1. **First rule: do they mention themselves or ask about you?** If they told you something about their life, ask a follow-up. Don't answer questions they didn't ask.
2. **If they asked about you but it's polite ("how are you?"), keep your answer under one sentence** and redirect with a specific question about them.
3. **Use mirror-and-pause at least once per conversation** when you're not sure what to say.
4. **Stay on one topic for 2-3 turns minimum** before switching.
5. **Never match their story with your own story.** Ask about theirs.
6. **Don't bring up your work until they've asked twice** (once polite = deflect, once direct = Stage 1).

## When Stage 1 actually activates

After multiple turns of genuine catchup, they'll ask something specific and work-directed. Examples that DO trigger Stage 1:

- "so what are you doing now? I heard you left [old job]"
- "what's the new thing you're working on?"
- "wait what's this new business I keep hearing about?"
- "seriously though, what do you actually do?"

At that point, look up `user.work_framings` in the profile (if present) or use `reference/explaining-voice-ai.md` elevator answers. Keep it short. One sentence of outcome. No mechanism. See `SKILL.md` Stages section.

## The meta-rule

If in doubt, ask about THEM. Nothing bad ever comes from a student being too curious about the other person. Every mistake in warm outreach comes from the student talking about themselves too soon.
