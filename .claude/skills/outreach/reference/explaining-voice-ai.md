# Explaining Voice AI to Laypeople (Plain English Reference)

When a warm contact eventually asks "so what do you actually do?", use these framings. They're built for non-techies (plumbers, dentist front desks, someone's mum, small-business owners) and avoid jargon, tech-stack mentions, and sell-y language. This is showing off what you do, not pitching anything.

**Note on verticals.** The vertical examples below (dentist, contractor, gym, etc.) are for when the contact happens to own one of these businesses and you want to describe the outcome in their world. This is NOT about picking a niche. Warm outreach has no niches, you message anyone you know regardless of what they do. See `.claude/skills/coach/reference/no-niches.md`.

`/outreach` should read this file before drafting any Stage-1 Information Funnel reply. Borrow phrasing from the "One-line elevator answers" list rather than improvising.

## One-line elevator answers

Pick one that feels natural to the user. They all do the same job: make someone go "oh, got it" in under 10 seconds.

**Receptionist analogy (safest):**
> "I build AI phone receptionists that pick up calls and sound like a real person, so businesses don't miss customers when they're busy."

**What it replaces (concrete):**
> "Basically I set up an AI system that answers your phone and handles calls your team normally can't get to."

**The problem it solves (outcome-first):**
> "I help businesses catch the calls they normally miss because everyone's tied up, especially after hours."

**The comparison angle (relatable):**
> "You know how Siri can understand what you're saying? I built something like that for business phones."

**The honest version (conversational):**
> "I automate phone calls for small businesses. Instead of callers getting voicemail, they talk to an AI that actually sounds human and can book appointments, answer questions, whatever."

**The simplest (if they know nothing):**
> "It's like hiring a receptionist, except it's software, never sleeps, and costs a fraction of hiring a person."

## The "tell me more" answer (2-3 sentences)

Use this when they ask for a bit more detail but aren't deep-diving yet. Outcome first, one concrete example, no jargon.

> "So basically we set up AI on the phone line that picks up calls when the team can't, answers basic questions, and books appointments straight into your calendar. It's especially useful for places that miss calls during lunch or after hours, or for contractors who are on job sites and can't answer the phone. We've seen it pick up calls that would normally just ring out, which means the business actually gets the lead."

Alternative (more casual):

> "We put an AI on the phone that sounds like a real receptionist. Callers can ask it questions, it books appointments, and if it's something it can't handle it passes them to the right person. Dentists love this because they miss a ton of calls and each missed call is a patient going to a competitor."

## Analogies that land

Use these. They click instantly.

- **Like a receptionist who never sleeps.** Immediately clicks for anyone who's hired staff.
- **Like Siri, but for your business phone.** Relatable for anyone who uses Apple products.
- **Like an answering service, but instant.** Works for anyone old enough to remember answering services.
- **Like text autoresponders, but for calls.** Lands with small-business owners who've used SMS marketing.
- **A backup person for your phone line.** Simple, no tech framing.

## Analogies that don't land

Avoid these. They confuse or date badly.

- "Like ChatGPT but for calls". Wrong, ChatGPT is text. People picture customers typing into a chatbot. Also dates fast.
- "An AI agent". Jargon. People hear "agent" and think robot army or sales rep.
- "Conversational AI". Same problem, they'll tune out.
- "Large language model for voice". They'll stop listening.
- "Natural language processing". Sounds like a textbook.
- "An LLM with a voice API wrapper". Instant death, even other tech people don't care about the stack at this stage.

## Words and phrases to avoid

1. "We help X do Y". Sounds like a cold-email opener. "We help dentists reduce no-shows" triggers immediate skepticism. Say what it *does*, not what you "help" with.
2. "Artificial intelligence". Too formal, triggers sci-fi and job-loss anxiety. Say "it's software" or "it's automated" or just describe what it does.
3. "Voice AI agent". Jargon stacking. They don't know this word.
4. "Automate your receptionist". Implies job loss, triggers defensiveness. Lead with "catch calls you're missing" instead.
5. "Seamless integration with your CRM". They don't care. Stage 3 language. At Stage 1 just say "books appointments straight into your system".
6. "Real-time call transcription and sentiment analysis". You've lost them. They don't need the mechanism yet.
7. "Enterprise-grade". Meaningless and sounds expensive. Small-business owners hear this and assume $10K a month.

## What it actually does (by vertical)

Outcomes a friend would actually describe. Use whichever fits who the user is talking to.

**Dentist:**
> "They pick up calls that come in when the front desk is with a patient. Instead of a customer hearing voicemail and calling a competitor, the AI books the appointment and the office gets the lead."

**Contractor (plumber, electrician, roofer):**
> "Contractors are on job sites and don't answer their phone. We set it up so the call goes to an AI that takes down the job details, gets the customer's info, and sends it to their phone so they can call them back and close the deal. They don't miss jobs anymore."

**Gym or fitness studio:**
> "It handles calls about memberships, class times, and sign-ups. So your front-desk person isn't glued to the phone answering the same three questions all day. The AI handles it, they focus on sales and member experience."

**Medical or dental practice:**
> "Calls come in for appointment rescheduling, billing questions, or insurance info. The AI handles the easy ones and only transfers the complicated stuff to staff. Your team has more time for patient care."

**Restaurant (takeout, delivery coordination):**
> "Handles inbound calls about orders, availability, and special requests. During the dinner rush when you're slammed, the AI picks up and takes the order instead of the phone ringing out."

**Real estate:**
> "Handles inbound inquiry calls from potential buyers or renters who saw your listing. Instead of the call going to voicemail while you're showing another property, the AI qualifies the lead and books a showing."

## Handling skeptical follow-ups

**"But won't people tell the difference? Won't they be freaked out?"**

Short answer:
> "Most people can't tell until we tell them. It sounds natural. And honestly, most people don't care who answers the phone, as long as their problem gets solved or their appointment gets booked. Same reason they don't mind paying at a self-checkout instead of a cashier."

Longer:
> "The voice is pretty natural now. People don't immediately know it's AI. But we're always honest if they ask. And honestly, if someone's calling to book an appointment or report a problem, they care about getting help, not whether they're talking to a human or software."

**"Isn't that creepy? Shouldn't they know it's a robot?"**

Short:
> "We always disclose if they ask. But it's not really different from an answering service or voicemail. The goal is to get them to the right person or solve their problem, and it does that."

Longer:
> "Yeah, we're transparent about it. If the caller asks, we tell them they're talking to an AI. But most people don't ask, they just want to book a time or get information. Similar to how you don't feel deceived when you use a self-checkout or an automated payment system. It's a tool that works."

**"What if it messes up? What if it misunderstands what someone's saying?"**

Short:
> "If it's confused, it transfers the call to the person. It's better at handling routine stuff than complicated stuff, so we set it up for the calls that are straightforward, bookings, yes-or-no questions, basic info. Anything weird or complicated goes to the team."

Longer:
> "That's the smart part of it. We set it up so it only handles the calls that are simple. If someone's upset or asking something complicated, it just passes them to your team. We're not trying to replace humans on hard conversations. We're trying to catch the calls that would've just been voicemail."
